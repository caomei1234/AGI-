## I. Two People Facing the Same Problem

A person has forward head posture, neck sore, searches online.

What does the first person get?—Tuck chin, stand against wall, buy neck pillow, do neck stretches, switch ergonomic chair, download posture correction app. He bookmarked fifteen videos, tried eight methods, none lasted more than two weeks.

The second person only asked one question: **what is pulling my head forward?**

The answer is eyes. Where the screen is, eyes chase; eyes chase, head follows. This is not a willpower problem, it's an automatically executed reflex circuit.

So the second person's solution has only one item: raise the screen two centimeters.

One method, permanent effect.

Where is the difference? Not who is smarter, not who has more information. **The difference is: the first person searches at the leaf level, the second person follows the vine all the way to the root.**

## II. Two Ways of Organizing Knowledge

Before you is a tree.

**Way one: collect leaves.** You pick each leaf, sort by color, sort by size, cover an entire wall. Your wall has a thousand leaves, you feel very knowledgeable. But when a new problem appears—a leaf you've never seen—you can only search from scratch again.

**Way two: know the trunk.** You only remember one thing: where does this tree grow from. Then you don't need to remember any single leaf, because all leaf positions are determined by the trunk. New leaf appears, you follow the branch and find it.

First way is called memory. Second way is called **root cause.**

Most people—including most AI—have a knowledge structure that is a wall covered with leaves. Looks very rich, very inefficient to use.

## III. Operational Definition of Root Cause

We don't give academic definitions. We give a definition you can verify on the spot:

> **The root cause is that thing—which, when you remove it, all branches above it automatically disappear.**

"Visual focus drives head forward" is the root cause of forward head posture, because if visual focus doesn't change, no matter how many stretches you do, how many chairs you switch, head will continue to move forward. And if you raise the screen to change line of sight, all symptoms automatically ease—you don't need to separately solve soreness, headache, shallow breathing, they are different leaves on the same vine.

**This is the first verification. But verifying within the same domain is not enough—you might have just touched a thick branch, not the true vine. There's a second path:**

> **If the same root cause in another completely different domain also explains a seemingly unrelated problem—you've probably touched the true vine.**

"Feedback loop breakage" in ecology explains coral reef bleaching and ocean collapse (first article). In AI engineering explains memory loss and five symptoms (second article). In resource domain explains phosphorus one-way extraction and loop breakage (third article). Three completely different domains, same structure, each has data support.

This is not analogy—analogy is "looks like." This is the same mechanism instantiated in different systems—causal chain traceable, intervention means verifiable.

**The signature of root cause is cross-domain mechanism consistency—not cross-domain metaphor similarity.**

## IV. Why Large Models Are Naturally Not Good at This

How is a large model's knowledge organized?

**By co-occurrence frequency.** It discovers "forward head posture" and "tuck chin" often appear together in massive text, so establishes association. It discovers "forward head posture" and "suboccipital muscles" occasionally appear together, also establishes association. All these associations lie on the same layer, with no distinction between trunk and branch.

It's like a library where books are not arranged by subject tree, but by "which books are often borrowed together." You can find the most popular combinations, but you can't find the skeleton of knowledge.

So what a large model can do is:

- Give you ten leaves (ten solutions)
- Tell you which leaf is most often mentioned (most popular answer)
- Even give you an explanation that looks like a root cause (but it's not sure if that's the true trunk, or just another thicker branch)

**The large model's default mode is counting melons, not following vines. But if you guide it the right way—like the four-step method in Section V of this article—it can be pulled onto the vine. This article itself is evidence: a piano teacher asked a question, a human and an AI together followed the vine to the root. The large model is not incapable of root cause analysis. It just doesn't do it—unless you put the method before it.**

## V. So What to Do: Root Cause Four-Step Method

The following four steps apply to both humans and AI. It's not a prompt technique—prompt techniques will become outdated. It's a thinking path, as long as knowledge exists, it is effective.

### Step One: Strip the Disguise

Facing any problem, first strip all domain terminology.

"Excessive muscle spindle density in suboccipital muscles causes tension signals to continuously transmit to brainstem"—strip it.

What's left? **"One sensor is continuously activated, the system cannot enter rest mode."**

This expression crosses domains. It simultaneously applies to: human nervous system, factory alarm system, phone background programs, an anxious person's brain.

**When you strip domain terminology, the problem suddenly becomes migratable.** This is the function of the disguise—it makes you feel this problem only belongs to a certain domain, but in fact it belongs to all domains.

Stripping the disguise is not for making analogies. It's for making the problem migratable. The purpose of migration is Step Three—cross-domain verification. If the problem after stripping the disguise also holds in another domain, you're one step closer to the true root cause.

### Step Two: Find the Minimal Sufficient Condition

Don't look for "all possible causes." Look for **that node that cannot be bypassed.**

Ask a test question: **"If I only change this one factor, everything else unchanged, will the problem automatically disappear?"**

- Raise screen → head automatically returns to correct position → eye position is root cause
- If only do chin-tuck training, screen height unchanged → head will still move forward → chin-tuck is not root cause

**Root cause is verified by "remove test," not by "how much evidence is added."**

In physical and engineering scenarios, remove test can be directly executed—raise screen, unplug, close valve. In psychological and social scenarios, it degrades to counterfactual reasoning—"if this factor didn't exist, would the problem disappear?"—weaker than physical intervention, but still stronger than "adding evidence" argumentation. It at least gives you a questionable structure, rather than a pile of scattered information.

### Step Three: Cross-Domain Verification

This is the most critical step, also the step most people skip.

The root cause you found, take it to another completely different domain and try it:

- You discover "visual focus drives head forward." Switch to cooking domain: when you're cooking, attention is all on the pot, heat control is very good, but prep table is a mess, kitchen looks like a battlefield after cooking. Root cause is not "not tidying"—it's **attention focus drives behavior to unilaterally偏移**, wherever you focus is good, other places automatically collapse.
- You discover "the more you try to control, the more anxious you become." Switch to management domain: is the team also crushed by excessive control? The more details the manager grabs, the more失控 the team becomes, because the desire for control itself becomes a new pressure source.

**If the root cause still holds in the new domain—you might have found that original vine that hasn't forked yet.**

If it doesn't hold in the new domain—what you found might just be a thick branch within a domain, not the true root.

### Step Four: Assemble, Don't Memorize

After finding the root cause, **don't remember the conclusion, remember the method.**

Conclusion is a leaf—"raising screen can solve forward head posture."
Method is the vine—"start from symptom, ask 'what is driving this behavior,' find minimal sufficient condition, cross-domain verify."

Next time you encounter a completely different problem—why does code become more bloated the more you write? Why do relationships become more tiring the more you maintain them? Why does weight rebound the more you lose it?—you don't need to remember each answer. You only need to start from the same position: **what is driving this system in the wrong direction? Remove it, does the rest automatically become right?**
